Part 1: Selfie: The Wrong Way vs. The Right Way

Instructions

Take a picture of your friend (or yourself) from close up. You get a typical distorted selfie image. Now step back several feet from your subject, zoom in, and take a second picture. Try to get the face in the second photo to be the same size as in the first photo.

If you've done things right, the second portrait should look much better than the first one. Think about why this is.

Why This Happens

When you take a close-up photo, you're very close to the subject, which creates strong perspective distortion. The features closer to the camera (like the nose) appear much larger relative to features farther away (like the ears). By stepping back and zooming in, you maintain the same framing while reducing this perspective distortion, resulting in a more natural-looking portrait.

Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Instructions

Let's repeat the same procedure in reverse, for an urban scene. Pick a nice view down a long street (or a walking path on campus), zoom in, and take a photo. Now, walk down the street in the direction of your first shot, and take a second photo without zoom, such that the scene in the two photos appears approximately the same size.

The first picture should look flattened, or compressed, compared to the second. Again, think about why.

Zoomed In (Compressed Perspective)

Zoomed in view from far away - notice the compressed, flattened appearance

No Zoom, Closer (Natural Perspective)

No zoom view from closer - shows natural perspective depth

Why This Happens

When you zoom in from far away, you're essentially looking through a narrower "window" at the scene. This compresses the apparent depth and makes objects at different distances appear closer together than they actually are. When you move closer without zooming, you maintain the natural perspective relationships between objects, making the scene appear more three-dimensional and realistic.

Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Instructions

In this part, we will be duplicating a classic film effect called the dolly zoom. It is a camera movement also known as the "Vertigo shot", after a famous scene in Hitchcock's Vertigo.

The idea is to simultaneously move the camera back while also zooming in. First, come up with a good setting for the shot (e.g., a scene with stuffed animals is one popular choice). Now take a few (4-8 or even more!) still photos while you move the camera back and zoom in, keeping the resulting image roughly the same size. Why This Effect Works

The dolly zoom creates a disorienting effect because it changes the perspective while maintaining the same framing. As you move back, the perspective naturally becomes more compressed (objects appear closer together), but as you zoom in, you're selecting a narrower view that would normally show more compressed perspective. These two effects work against each other, creating a unique visual experience where the background appears to "move" relative to the foreground subject.
